Research & Scientific Interests

A few words about my research.

As part of the Aramis Lab, located at the Brain & Spine Institute at the Pitié Salpétrière, in Paris, my PhD thesis aims at exploring new ways to understand the brain evolution, in particular the dynamics of neurodegenerative diseases, thanks to Machine Learning tools.


The goals of my research are three-fold:
  • Get a better understanding of the spatio-temporal patterns of brain alterations, especially by developping numerical models of the disease progression, based on longitudinal data,
  • Characterize individual patterns of progression by analyzing the impact of cofactors (gender, genes, socio-demographical information), and, by predicting individual diagnosis and disease stage, up to 5 years in advance,
  • Simulate virtual cohorts of patients that reproduce the characteristics of real longitudinal cohorts, to, first, overpass anonymization requirements, and, in a second time, enhance classic Machine Learning learners


To this end, I have proposed multiple methological enhancements to existing methods (see Publications). Most of them rely on a variety of tools and software I have developped :
  • Leasp: Estimation of the group-average and individual spatio-temporal trajectories. Well suited for the estimation of spatially distributed data. C++. Website.
  • Leaspy: Python version of Leasp, designed for vector-like observations (biomarkers, cognitive scores, image extracted features, ...). It includes methods to simulate virtual patients. Python. New release soon.
  • Digital Brain: Web application to vizualise the group-average and invidiaul evolution of the cortical thickness, the PET-FDG, the hippocampus mesh and the cognitive scores, during the course of Alzheimer's Disease. Digital-Brain
  • Longitudinal Prediction: Deep learning based methods to predict a selected variable with the ability to add virtual patients, in a longitudinal setting. Python. New release soon.
